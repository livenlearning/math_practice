Grade 3 Fractions – Lesson 1

What Fractions Mean: Understanding parts of a whole, and what the top and bottom numbers tell you.

1. Big Idea: Fractions Tell a Story

Concept

A fraction is a way to talk about equal parts of a whole.

  • The denominator (bottom number) tells how many equal parts the whole is cut into.
  • The numerator (top number) tells how many of those parts you have.

Example: 3/4 means “3 out of 4 equal parts.”

Imagine a cookie, a pizza, or a game level bar at the bottom of a screen. Fractions tell how much you have out of the whole.

3. Which Fraction Is Bigger?

Compare

For each pair, decide which fraction is bigger. Think about which gives you more of the same-sized cookie!

1. Which is bigger: 1/4 or 1/8?

2. Which is bigger: 2/3 or 1/2?

3. Which is bigger: 3/5 or 3/10?

Choose answers for each question, then click Check Answers.

Hint: If the pieces are the same size, having more pieces means a bigger fraction. If the numerator is the same, having fewer, bigger pieces (smaller denominator) usually means more.

4. Word Problems

Stories

Read each story, think about the fractions, and decide who has more or how far someone went. Then check the answer.

1. Cookie Sharing

Teddy and Ada each have a cookie of the same size. Teddy eats 1/2 of his cookie. Ada eats 1/3 of her cookie. Who ate more cookie?

Teddy ate more. Half of a cookie (1/2) is bigger than one third (1/3) of the same cookie.

2. Playground Loop

Ada runs 1/3 of the playground loop. Teddy runs 1/6 of the same loop. Who ran farther?

Ada ran farther. One third (1/3) of the loop is more than one sixth (1/6) of the same loop.

3. Pizza Party

There are 2 pizzas of the same size at a party. One pizza is cut into 4 equal slices. The other pizza is cut into 8 equal slices. If you eat 2 slices from each pizza, from which pizza did you eat more?

You ate more from the pizza with 4 slices. 2 out of 4 slices is 2/4 = 1/2 of the pizza. 2 out of 8 slices is 2/8 = 1/4 of the pizza. Half is more than one fourth.

5. Reflection

Think

Talk or write about these questions:

  1. In your own words, what does the denominator tell you?
  2. In your own words, what does the numerator tell you?
  3. Would you rather have 1/2 of a candy bar or 1/3 of the same candy bar? Why?
  4. Can a fraction ever be bigger than 1? What would that mean?
